# Simon — Confessions of the Pricing Man

**TL;DR:** Price is the single most powerful lever on profit, the most under-managed one, and the truest expression of how much value you create. Pricing power — not market share — is the goal.

## Core principles
- **Price is the strongest profit lever.** A 1% price improvement, holding volume, typically beats a 1% cut in costs or a 1% volume gain, because it flows almost entirely to the bottom line. Yet it gets the least management attention.
- **Profit, not revenue or share, is the objective.** Chasing volume via low price destroys profit; price wars are usually lose-lose.
- **Value is the only honest basis for price.** Price should reflect the customer's *perceived* value — and perception can be shaped, but not faked indefinitely.
- **Elasticity matters even in B2B.** Demand responds to price; you must understand the curve before you move. In B2B the response is lumpier (fewer, larger deals) but real.
- **Pricing power > market power.** Warren Buffett's test — the ability to raise price without losing business to competitors — is the clearest sign of a strong franchise.
- **Avoid cost-plus and me-too pricing.** Both ignore the customer's value entirely.

## How it informs our product
- The "1% price = outsized profit" math is the **CFO-facing ROI narrative** for the discount-leakage diagnostic: recovered realization drops nearly straight to profit.
- **Price realization (actual/list)** is the operational proxy for Simon's pricing-power thesis — a falling ratio is eroding power; we make it visible and trended.
- Elasticity / scenario modeling (pillar 3) operationalizes his demand-curve point for **lumpy B2B deal data**.
- Reframes the sales pitch: stop celebrating bookings, **start measuring whether we hold price** — discount governance follows.

## Watch-outs / where it's weak
- B2B elasticity is genuinely hard to estimate from sparse, confounded deal data — be honest about uncertainty bands rather than implying precision.
- The book is high-level and anecdotal; it motivates the *why*, not the *how* — pair it with Nagle for mechanics.
