# Simon-Kucher — Discount Governance

**TL;DR:** Discounting destroys margin quietly and at scale. The fix is governance — clear price authority, approval thresholds tied to value given/received, and instrumentation so concessions are visible, justified, and traded for something.

## Core principles
- **Discount leakage is systemic, not anecdotal.** Across portfolios, unmanaged discretionary discounting is one of the largest and least-tracked margin drains.
- **Authority must be explicit.** Define who can approve what discount depth, with escalating thresholds (rep → manager → deal desk → finance). Anything above policy needs sign-off and a logged rationale.
- **Trade concessions, never give them.** Every discount should buy something back: a longer term, prepayment, a reference, a higher commitment, reduced scope.
- **Pricing committee / deal desk owns governance.** A cross-functional body sets policy, reviews exceptions, and closes the loop between what's approved and what's invoiced.
- **Govern the price waterfall.** List → invoice → pocket price: track every leak (volume, promo, payment-term, rebate, off-invoice) so the *pocket* margin is managed, not just headline list.

## How it informs our product
- This is the **governance/approval-workflow pillar (#6)** and the analytical core of the discount-leakage diagnostic — we quantify the waterfall and the dollar leakage.
- **Win-rate-by-discount-band** is the evidence base for setting approval thresholds: bands that win without deep discounts expose over-discounting.
- Deal/quote guidance can **enforce trade-for-concession rules** in real time — recommend a term/commitment ask whenever a discount crosses a threshold.
- Every above-policy approval becomes a **logged, auditable record** — the "system of record for pricing decisions" promise, with explainability for finance.

## Watch-outs / where it's weak
- Governance that's too rigid kills deal velocity (time-to-quote, quote-to-close suffer); thresholds must be calibrated, not maximally strict.
- Firm methodology is consulting IP, not a published standard — treat specifics as directional and validate against the partner's own data.
