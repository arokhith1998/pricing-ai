# Nagle — The Strategy and Tactics of Pricing

**TL;DR:** Price to the value the customer receives, not to cost or to competitors. Quantify that value (Economic Value to Customer), manage the customer's reference price, and segment so each buyer pays in line with the value they get.

## Core principles
- **Economic Value to Customer (EVC).** Value = the cost of the best available alternative (*reference value*) plus the monetized worth of everything your offer does better or worse (*differentiation value*). EVC is the ceiling on a rational buyer's WTP.
- **Reference price drives perception.** Buyers judge a price relative to an anchor (a competitor, the prior price, a published list). Manage the anchor deliberately — discounts reset it downward.
- **Price segmentation.** Use fences (volume, channel, commitment, feature tier, customer type) so different segments pay different prices for value received — without arbitrage between segments.
- **Three strategic postures:** *skim* (high price, value-insensitive early adopters), *penetration* (low price to win share where buyers are price-sensitive and switching is sticky), *neutral* (price isn't the primary lever). Choose deliberately.
- **Value communication beats discounting.** If price exceeds perceived value, fix the value message before cutting price.

## How it informs our product
- **EVC is the analytical backbone of a defensible reference price** in the diagnostic — the benchmark against which we measure price realization (actual/list) and flag leakage.
- **Reference-price logic explains discount drift:** every concession resets the customer's anchor, so the platform should treat repeated discounting as anchor erosion, not isolated deals.
- **Segmentation fences feed win-rate-by-discount-band:** if a band wins regardless of discount, the fence (not the rep) is mispriced.
- Deal/quote guidance should surface the **value gap (EVC minus quoted price)** so reps argue value instead of conceding price.

## Watch-outs / where it's weak
- EVC requires credible inputs on the customer's alternative and differentiation worth — hard to estimate and easy to inflate; garbage in, garbage out.
- The framework assumes a fairly rational buyer; emotional, political, and procurement dynamics in enterprise deals can dominate.
