# Ramanujam — Monetizing Innovation

**TL;DR:** Design the product around what customers will pay for — not the reverse. Have the pricing conversation early, repeatedly, with real willingness-to-pay (WTP) data, before sinking engineering cost.

## Core principles
- **Price before product.** WTP is a product-design input, not a launch afterthought. Most "innovation failures" are monetization failures, not engineering failures.
- **Four failure archetypes:** *feature shock* (overstuffed, undifferentiated), *minivation* (underpriced incremental wins), *hidden gem* (a high-WTP feature never commercialized), *undead* (a product nobody wanted, killed slowly).
- **Willingness-to-pay research is doable.** Ask about value and price ranges directly and early; use the data to cut/keep features and to segment.
- **Segment by needs and WTP, not demographics.** Different segments value different things — build packages (good/better/best, leader/filler/killer features) around that.
- **Choose a monetization model deliberately:** the model (subscription, usage, dynamic, per-outcome, freemium, etc.) is a design decision with its own WTP profile.
- **The leaky bucket:** discounting, weak monetization, and price erosion silently drain the value you created. Plug leaks before chasing new revenue.

## How it informs our product
- The **leaky bucket is literally our wedge.** The discount-leakage diagnostic quantifies one of Ramanujam's biggest silent profit drains in dollar terms.
- **WTP-by-segment** maps onto value-metric selection and win-rate-by-discount-band: where realized price diverges from WTP, leakage is structural, not deal-specific.
- **Leader/filler/killer packaging** logic feeds the packaging-architecture and price-optimization pillars — guidance can flag when a discount is really a packaging mismatch.
- **Price-before-product** framing is the pitch to founders: we turn monetization from a gut call into an instrumented system of record.

## Watch-outs / where it's weak
- Stated-WTP surveys overstate willingness; triangulate against actual closed-deal behavior (which is exactly what our diagnostic supplies).
- The book is consulting-flavored and prescriptive; the four archetypes are illustrative, not a rigorous taxonomy.
